Introduction

The dark spots on her legs tell a story.  The pattern of discoloration on her skin confirms she had been burned with a cigarette lighter.  Reports say she passed out from the pain.  Dumped in a ditch, left to die.  When she was discovered, she was in a daze, shaking. 

She was three years old. 

Her name is Terri.  She’s my daughter.

To some people, Terri and others like her are considered the throwaway kids.  They were abandoned, abused, and traumatized at an early age.  Now that they’re older, they suffer from all kinds of behavior problems.  Nobody wants to adopt problem kids like these.

We did. 

This is our story—the story of how God inspired Possum Trot, Texas to take on 72 of the toughest kids from the foster care system, and adopt them as our very own.  It’s also the story of how God has used our little community of 300 folks to touch the conscience of a nation.

Our kids didn’t come to use as cute little Gerber babies.  They came to us rough, right off the street, and right into our homes, where they lied to us, they cheated us, and they stole from us.  They were—and still can be--masters of manipulation.  It’s how they learned how to survive.  Our kids were abandoned by their mamas, left at home with nothing to eat.  They had to sneak food from the store just to put something in their tummies.  Some of our kids were beaten by drunk daddies.  Some were raped.  These are little children I’m talking about. 

 

And that’s where the heroes come in.  You’re going to meet some of them.  These aren’t celebrities on TV.  These are real life heroes.  Take the Browns, for instance.  Here’s a grandmother and grandfather—with kids already grown—who decided to adopt triplets!  Two year old triplets!  You’ll also meet Diann Sparks—a single working mom--who adopted two boys as her own.  And the Lathan’s—wait till you hear what happened to them.  But I’m getting ahead of myself. 

You might have heard parts of our story already.  God blessed us with opportunities to appear on NBC News, Good Morning America, and CBS.  My wife and I even got to be on The Oprah Winfrey Show.  You might’ve seen us written up in Reader’s Digest, or People magazine, or Family Circle, or Southern Living. 

It’s quite a blessing.  It’s quite a story.  It’s the Miracle of Possum Trot, Texas. 

 

Donna Martin

 

I can still see my Mama sitting in her rocking chair here on our front porch.  I can still hear her humming.  I can still feel her arms around me as a little girl as I sat quietly in her lap, my head against her breast, listening. 

In moments like these, lessons were inscribed on my heart—lessons that inspired me to one day adopt little ones who were strangers to love. 

 

Well Done, Son

originally written for The Navigators

            VIDEO:  A man in his sixties walks through the woods. 

When I was little, my Father and I would take these long walks in the woods behind our house. 

Father had a quiet voice.  He didn’t say much.  But when he did, I listened carefully.  Because his words were like a light to me.  Whatever dark doubts were clouding my mind, whatever shadows of fear I faced, all I needed to know was that Father was with me.  All I needed to hear from him were three words.  “Well done, Son.” 

            VIDEO:  Shots of the man walking down the path, an empty space beside him.

           

He would quietly put his hand on my shoulder.  I didn’t need to see his face.  I could feel his smile.  And everything was all right. 

VIDEO:  Sitting on a log, an empty place next to him. 

 

When I went off to college, I had less time for those walks with Father.  I was busy.  I had classes and work and friends.  I never heard Father complain.  But I could tell he missed me and our walks together.

 

After college, I decided to become a mentor.  Because of my Father.  Because I wanted others to experience those times I had enjoyed with my Father. 

            VIDEO:  Hands pick up a journal, embossed with the Navigators logo

 

Now that I’m older, I still take long walks in the woods.  Whatever dark doubts are clouding my mind, whatever shadows of fear I face, I still long to hear those three words from Father. “Well done, Son.” 

I can still feel Father’s hand on my shoulder.  And I don’t need to see his face.  I can tell…he’s smiling.  And everything’s all right.   

One day, maybe not far from now, I’m going to get to see him.  And on that day, there won’t be any more doubts.  There won’t be any more fear.  And yet, even then, something inside me will want to hear him say it again.

I long for that day when he’ll put his hand on my shoulder.  When I’ll see his face.  Smiling.  And he says, “Well done, good and faithful servant.  Well done…Son.”

And everything will be all right.                    

 (inc. Caring for Others, Developing Healthy Relationships)
